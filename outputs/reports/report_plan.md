# Digital Minds Field Map — Report Plan

**Version:** Draft for review
**Date:** 2026-03-18
**Status:** Pre-writing — structure and approach to be confirmed before drafting begins

---

## 1. Purpose and framing

### What this report is

This report accompanies the Digital Minds Field Map — a systematic directory of organisational actors in the digital minds space. The map itself is designed as a web resource (modelled on AISafety.com's field map): a structured, searchable directory. The report is the analytical companion: it explains what was found, what the findings mean, and what the field's structure reveals that no list alone can show.

The two are designed to be read together but usable independently. Someone arriving at the web directory gets orientation without the report. Someone reading the report gets analysis grounded in a reproducible dataset.

### What this report is not

It is not a systematic literature review. It maps organisations, not publications. It does not assess the quality of any organisation's research or advocate for particular positions within the field. It is descriptive and structural, with analysis of what the structure means.

### Audience

Two primary audiences, pulling in slightly different directions:

**New entrants** — graduate students, early-career researchers, professionals considering work in the digital minds space. They need: what the field looks like, who the key actors are, where the action is, how the zones relate.

**Policy professionals** — government advisors, grant-makers, think-tank staff engaging with digital minds as a policy-relevant topic. They need: institutional structure, funding landscape, geographic spread, where the field is mature vs. nascent, how it relates to adjacent spaces (AI safety, AI governance).

The report should be readable for both without requiring prior familiarity with consciousness science or philosophy of mind. Where technical terms are introduced, they are briefly defined. Jargon is minimised.

### Precedents and analogues

- **AISafety.com / AISafety.world:** Visual field maps without accompanying analytical report. Our report fills this gap.
- **Rethink Priorities Welfare of Digital Minds Research Agenda (2024):** Hierarchical, question-driven, policy-facing — a useful structural model for how to organise findings.
- **Caviola & Saad, "Futures with Digital Minds" (2025):** Expert survey report — accessible, evidence-grounded, practitioner-facing. Sets the right tone register.
- **Caviola, "Social Science of Digital Minds: Research Agenda" (2024):** Question-driven framing of a nascent field — relevant model for how to introduce a space to non-specialists.

Our report sits closest to a **think-tank field review** — systematic data collection, analytical write-up, practitioner audience, honest about uncertainty. The closest analogue in the broader landscape is the kind of field mapping report produced by Founders Pledge or Open Philanthropy for cause area assessments, but with explicit reproducibility infrastructure (corpus, protocol, methodology log).

## 2. Style

Apply the PRISM STYLE.md throughout. Key principles:

- **British English** throughout.
- **Affirmative claims.** Say what the field *is*, not what it is not. Avoid "not X, but Y" constructions.
- **Precision over comfort.** If a finding is tentative, say so with specificity: "the early data suggest..." rather than "evidence clearly shows..." or the opposite retreat into hedging abstraction.
- **Hold tensions open.** Where the field presents genuine contradictions — institutional separation from AI safety while drawing on AI safety infrastructure; the Anthropic/Microsoft divide; the geographic asymmetry — do not paper over them. Name them and let them stand.
- **No Claude-isms.** Avoid "landscape" (ironic given the genre), "crucial", "nuanced", "delve", "multifaceted", false-depth triplets, performative hedging openers ("it is worth noting that..."), and grandiose reframes.
- **First person where appropriate.** "We argue" and "we suggest" are clearer and more honest than impersonal constructions.
- **Vary rhythm.** Short sentences for simple thoughts. Longer sentences when the syntax does intellectual work.

## 3. Report structure

The report has four parts. Total target length: **20–25 pages** (excluding appendices). An executive briefing of **4–6 pages** will be produced separately, drawing on Part 1 and Part 3.

---

### Part 1 — Orientation (approx. 4 pages)

#### 1.1 Introduction (~1.5 pages)

Purpose: situate the reader. Answer: what is the digital minds field, why does mapping it matter, and what does this report do?

- Define the field briefly: the interdisciplinary space concerned with whether AI systems might be conscious, sentient, or moral patients, and what follows from that.
- Explain the purpose of a field map at this moment: the field is growing rapidly but lacks public institutional visibility. New entrants and policy professionals need orientation that no individual publication or directory currently provides.
- State what the report covers (organisations) and what it does not (individuals, publications, quality assessment).
- Briefly note the relationship to the PRISM individual researcher network.

*Tone note:* Open with the intellectual stakes, not with methodology. The reader should immediately understand why the field is worth mapping, before they learn how.

#### 1.2 Methodology (~2.5 pages)

Purpose: establish reproducibility and transparency. Concise but complete.

Subsections:
- **Scope and eligibility:** what counts as in-scope; the inclusion/adjacent/excluded taxonomy; the funder-specific criterion; the PI lab granularity rule; the individual researcher exclusion policy.
- **Zone taxonomy:** the six zones with one-paragraph descriptions; the decision to use topical/disciplinary zone as primary organising principle with org_type and function as filterable secondary dimensions; the removal of Zone 7 (AI Safety Adjacent) and what that decision revealed.
- **Search strategy:** four primary rounds plus ongoing additions; seed sources used; snowball procedure; how geographic expansion was approached.
- **Limitations:** anglophone search bias; Chinese-language gap; private sector below visibility threshold; survey date (field is growing rapidly — entries added after 2026-03-18 are not captured); known geographic absences confirmed via targeted search rather than assumed.

*Format:* Mostly narrative with one summary table (search rounds). This section should be precise without being exhausting. The full protocol is in the appendix.

### Part 2 — The Field (approx. 12 pages)

One section per zone. Each section follows a consistent internal structure:
1. **What this zone is** (~1 paragraph): define the zone's scope and role in the field.
2. **Key actors** (~2–3 paragraphs): describe the most significant organisations, with analysis of what they reveal about the zone's structure — not just a list.
3. **Zone-level findings** (~1–2 paragraphs): what does the zone look like as a whole? Density, maturity, geographic spread, notable absences.

Zones are presented in order of their relationship to the field's intellectual foundations — moving from the empirical/scientific base outward to the applied and policy-facing.

#### 2.1 Zone 1 — Consciousness Science (~2 pages)

The foundational zone: empirical research on consciousness in biological systems. These are the labs whose theoretical frameworks (IIT, GWT, RPT, AST, HOT, predictive processing, free energy principle) the digital minds field imports directly.

Key points to make:
- Zone 1 is the most densely populated zone (22 included organisations) and the most geographically distributed.
- Theoretical pluralism: five major competing frameworks, each with dedicated institutional homes in the corpus. The TWCF adversarial collaboration programme (COGITATE, ETHOS) is the most significant structural feature — it treats the theoretical competition as a scientific problem to be resolved empirically.
- Most Zone 1 labs do not explicitly study AI consciousness. The frameworks are relevant; the application is largely happening in Zones 2 and 3. This gap is itself a finding.
- The Graziano Lab and Monash M3CS are notable exceptions — explicit AI application.
- Templeton (TWCF) as the zone's primary infrastructure funder: early mover (Araya grant, 2018), the $30M ARC programme. One confirmed direct AI consciousness grant; the zone otherwise funded through general consciousness science infrastructure.

#### 2.2 Zone 2 — Philosophy of Mind and Moral Status (~1.5 pages)

The normative and conceptual zone: academic philosophy groups working on consciousness, subjective experience, moral patienthood. Provides the evaluative frameworks that Zone 3 applies.

Key points:
- Smaller than Zone 1 (9 included) but highly networked — many Zone 2 researchers are co-authors of Zone 3 outputs. Chalmers, Block, Birch, Shevlin are the key connective nodes.
- NYU is a notable cluster: Mind Brain and Consciousness Center (Chalmers/Block) + CMEP (Sebo) + Wild Animal Welfare Program + CEAP — a convergence of consciousness philosophy, AI welfare, and animal ethics in a single institutional geography.
- The Berggruen/After Consciousness project is a distinctive entry: conceptual engineering rather than standard consciousness philosophy. Cappelen's project questions whether "consciousness" is even the right organising concept — a meta-level intervention that sits at an angle to the rest of Zone 2.
- LCFI (Cambridge) is the most explicitly policy-facing Zone 2 entry — the Leverhulme funding, Shevlin's work on non-human consciousness, the Digital Minds Fellowship partnership with PRISM.

#### 2.3 Zone 3 — AI Consciousness and Welfare Research (~2.5 pages)

The core zone: organisations where digital minds is the primary research focus.

Key points:
- The youngest zone and the fastest-growing. Most entries founded 2022–2025. Several (CIMC, Reciprocal Research, CoSI) are very early-stage.
- Rethink Priorities' Welfare of Digital Minds team is the most developed research programme — the Digital Consciousness Model is the most systematic attempt at empirical assessment of the question to date.
- Eleos AI is the primary welfare-assessment infrastructure: the only organisation explicitly conducting model welfare assessments, with direct Anthropic partnership.
- The zone has a notable methodological split: empirical assessment (RP, Eleos, Reciprocal Research) vs. philosophical/normative (CMEP, CoSI, CLR, CIMC).
- Geographic concentration: heavy USA bias (9 of 17 included), with UK cluster (PRISM, CLR, Cambridge Digital Minds, LCFI bridge). Japan (Araya) is the only non-Anglophone primary research org.
- Funding surge in 2025: Longview Digital Sentience Fund, Macroscopic Ventures, Navigation Fund Consortium round. The field has moved from grant scarcity to comparative abundance in under two years.

#### 2.4 Zone 4 — Animal and Broader Sentience (~1.5 pages)

The empirical analogue zone: sentience research on non-human animals provides the closest methodological model for AI consciousness assessment.

Key points:
- The LSE Coller Centre (Birch) is the key bridge institution — Birch co-authored Butlin et al. 2023 and his precautionary framework for animal sentience is being explicitly extended to AI.
- Jeff Sebo's NYU cluster spans Zones 2 and 4, bridging moral philosophy and applied welfare science.
- Sentient Futures (formerly AI for Animals): the primary community organisation explicitly bridging animal welfare and digital minds; its 2025 rebrand is a signal of the field's convergence.
- Wild animal welfare orgs (WAI, Fish Welfare Initiative, Insect Welfare Research Society) are included as the lower threshold of moral consideration — the logic of including organisms at the minimal-sentience boundary is the same logic that animates AI welfare reasoning.

#### 2.5 Zone 5 — AI Lab Programs (~1.5 pages)

Industry engagement: the smallest zone in terms of included entries, but arguably the highest-stakes.

Key points:
- A four-way typology emerges: **formal program** (Anthropic), **individual research output** (Google DeepMind), **team-based research with explicit welfare output** (Google Research Paradigms of Intelligence), **private research company** (Conscium).
- Anthropic's Model Welfare Program remains the only formal, named, publicly committed welfare program at a major lab — Kyle Fish, the first full-time AI welfare researcher at any major lab, is a field-building milestone in itself.
- The Microsoft contrast: Suleyman's "dangerous" framing is the clearest articulation of industry resistance. The divide is not just about positions on AI consciousness; it reflects different assessments of what publicly crediting AI experience does to user behaviour and regulatory exposure.
- OpenAI's dissolved Model Behavior team is documented as a structural negative — a named function absorbed into the broader organisation without successor program.
- Google Research (Paradigms of Intelligence, led by Blaise Agüera y Arcas) is a notable entry: led by a Google VP with his own history of writing on AI sentience, producing explicit welfare research output alongside a 2026 book on AI welfare.

#### 2.6 Zone 6 — Policy and Governance (~1.5 pages)

The emergent policy layer: the smallest included zone, reflecting the field's early stage on the policy dimension.

Key points:
- Four included organisations, spanning regulatory readiness (Harder Problem Project, Sentience Readiness Index), legal/academic (Institute for Law and AI), standards development (AI Rights Institute), and advocacy (UFAIR). Together they represent the full range from formal policy-making to movement building.
- The zone's sparseness is a finding, not a gap in the search. Digital minds has not yet developed the policy infrastructure that AI governance or AI safety have. The Sentience Readiness Index (30+ countries) is the most systematic attempt to map governmental readiness for the question.
- Two adjacent organisations are documented as significant absences: GovAI and CAIS (no digital minds workstreams), reflecting the structural separation from AI safety/governance documented in the Zone 7 finding.

### Part 3 — Cross-cutting Analysis (approx. 6 pages)

The section that moves from description to interpretation. Where Part 2 presents each zone's internal structure, Part 3 asks what the corpus as a whole reveals.

#### 3.1 The funder landscape (~1.5 pages)

- Two distinct funding eras: the Templeton era (2018–2024, consciousness science infrastructure) and the EA/longtermist surge (2025, direct digital minds grants).
- Templeton as early mover and infrastructure funder: the TWCF ARC programme built Zone 1; the Araya grant (2018) was the first direct AI consciousness investment. No comparable second wave from Templeton post-2021.
- 2025 saw the first concentrated philanthropic push specifically targeting digital minds: Longview (Digital Sentience Fund), Macroscopic Ventures, Navigation Fund, Coefficient Giving. The LTFF Robert Long grant (2023) seeded Butlin et al. 2023 — a small early investment with outsized field impact.
- The funder landscape has moved from scarcity to relative abundance in under two years. The question of whether this produces durable institutional infrastructure or a bubble of early-stage organisations is genuinely open.

#### 3.2 Field-building infrastructure (~1 page)

- Training: three major fellowship programmes (Cambridge Digital Minds Fellowship, Neuromatch AI Sentience Scholars, PIVOTAL/SPAR/MATS tracks). The field is beginning to build a pipeline.
- Media: four peer-reviewed journals (JAIC, Neuroscience of Consciousness, PhiMiSci, JCS) plus Digital Minds Newsletter and public-facing venues (Noema, Aeon). JAIC, launched 2020, is the dedicated venue for the AI-consciousness interface.
- Community: ASSC, AMCS, EA Forum Digital Minds community. The ASSC (est. 1994) provides the most established professional community infrastructure.
- The field-building layer is more developed than might be assumed for an emerging area — but pipeline programmes are mostly less than two years old.

#### 3.3 Geographic distribution (~1 page)

- The Anglophone core is pronounced: USA (approximately 40% of included organisations), UK (approximately 20%), Australia, Canada.
- An East Asian cluster is emerging and partially institutionalised: Japan (Araya, CHAIN, RIKEN CBS), China (ShanghaiTech), Hong Kong (AI & Humanity Lab via Berggruen).
- Confirmed absences: Africa, Latin America, India, Southeast Asia (outside Japan/HK). Targeted searches confirm these as genuine field absences rather than search failures.
- The Berggruen/HKU relationship is the most deliberate attempt at geographic bridge-building: the China Fellowship, bilingual essay prize, and After Consciousness workshop in Hong Kong represent a conscious institutional strategy for extending the conversation into Chinese-language academic cultures.
- Geographic asymmetry is a field-structure finding with implications for whose frameworks, whose welfare concerns, and whose policy priorities shape the field.

#### 3.4 Structural relationship to AI safety (~1 page)

- A systematic search found no AI safety organisation with a dedicated digital minds workstream. CAIS, CHAI, GovAI, FLI: all adjacent. The field is institutionally separate from mainstream AI safety.
- The separation is not purely intellectual — the two fields share some researchers and some funding sources (Coefficient Giving funds both) — but the organisational structures are distinct.
- The community/onboarding zone is the clearest exception: PIVOTAL, SPAR, and MATS are AI safety training programmes that have added digital minds mentor tracks. These represent the primary institutional bridge.
- The policy implication: digital minds questions are unlikely to be addressed by existing AI governance infrastructure. Dedicated institutional capacity is required.

#### 3.5 The industry divide (~1 page)

- Zone 5 presents a sharper institutional contrast than any other zone. Anthropic's formal program vs. Microsoft's explicit "dangerous" framing represents not just different positions on AI consciousness, but different assessments of what publicly crediting AI experience does — to users, to regulators, to the companies themselves.
- DeepMind's Lerchner preprint (2026, anti-functionalist, explicitly invokes the "AI welfare trap") is philosophically serious but institutionally cautious — published by an individual researcher, not a lab program. The contrast with Anthropic's institutional commitment is a data point about how labs are managing the question.
- OpenAI's dissolved Model Behavior team, Google Research's explicit welfare research output, Conscium's private research: together these suggest a field where industry engagement is real but unevenly distributed and still finding its institutional form.

### Part 4 — Implications and Open Questions (approx. 2–3 pages)

Short, honest, without false resolution. Three audiences explicitly addressed.

#### 4.1 For new entrants

- Where are the entry points? Training programmes, fellowship tracks, the Digital Minds Fellowship at Cambridge, Neuromatch AI Sentience Scholars.
- What does the zone structure imply for positioning? The empirical core (Zone 3) is young and has clear demand for researchers with technical and philosophical backgrounds. Zone 1–2 bridge work (applying existing consciousness science to AI) is less institutionally developed than the direct digital minds research.
- Where are the gaps? Policy (Zone 6), international (non-Anglophone), and technical empirical approaches to AI consciousness assessment.

#### 4.2 For policy professionals

- The field has an emerging policy layer (Zone 6) but lacks the institutional density that AI safety and AI governance have built over a decade. Engaging early means engaging with a field still forming its own infrastructure.
- The Sentience Readiness Index and AI Rights Institute are the most developed policy-facing resources. The Harder Problem Project's model legislation is the closest to formal policy-facing output.
- The structural separation from AI governance means digital minds questions are unlikely to be addressed adequately by existing regulatory frameworks. Dedicated policy attention is warranted.

#### 4.3 Open questions the map cannot answer

- **Informal networks:** The map captures institutional actors; the field's informal network of individual researchers (captured partially in the companion PRISM researcher network) is not visible here.
- **Quality and output:** Inclusion reflects organisational activity, not quality of research. Some included organisations are early-stage with limited output; others are producing the field's foundational literature.
- **Trajectory:** A field map is a snapshot. The digital minds field is growing quickly; the corpus should be re-run at 12-month intervals to track institutional change.
- **Whether the field coheres:** The six zones represent disciplines with distinct research traditions, methodologies, and communities. Whether the digital minds field constitutes a unified intellectual project or a loose coalition of adjacent concerns is a genuine open question that a field map cannot resolve.

## 6. Confirmed design decisions

*(Agreed 2026-03-18)*

1. **Part 4 tone:** Remain descriptive and largely unopinionated in this draft. The data should speak. Where the findings suggest clear directions, light suggestive comments are welcome, but no strong recommendations. Once the draft exists and the patterns are visible, a second pass can introduce more editorial voice where warranted.

2. **Zone section tone:** Analytical narrative as the primary register. The goal is a document that is genuinely readable and reflective — useful to policy professionals and funders arriving from outside the field, and equally useful to organisational leaders inside the field who want a structural read of where effort and resources are or are not concentrated. Fewer entries cited with more analysis of what they represent, rather than a comprehensive survey of every inclusion.

3. **PRISM self-inclusion:** Flag clearly and briefly in the introduction — one or two sentences. No need for extended treatment. The report will be reviewed by partner organisations; conflicts of interest are not a significant concern given the descriptive nature of the analysis.

4. **Executive briefing:** Included as the opening section of the main report (not a separate document). Drafts to target approximately 20 pages total. Sections that prove most analytically rich will be expanded in revision.

5. **Publication:** Full report and interactive stakeholder map hosted on PRISM's website. Report subtitle: *Survey Report* — signalling shared methodology with other PRISM systematic distillation projects in development. Executive summary cross-posted to EA Forum and other relevant venues. Assumed audience for the full report: no prior familiarity with the field required, but some comfort with research-adjacent writing.
