# Digital Minds: A Field Map
## Survey Report — First Draft

**Published by:** PRISM — Partnership for Research Into Sentient Machines
**Date:** 2026
**Status:** First draft — internal review

*This report was produced by PRISM. PRISM is itself included in the corpus as an active organisational actor in the field; this is disclosed in the methodology section and in the dataset.*

---

# Executive Briefing

The digital minds field is concerned with whether AI systems might be conscious, sentient, or moral patients. In under a decade, it has grown from a niche philosophical speculation into an institutionally substantial research area. This report maps the organisations that constitute that field as of early 2026.

This report is intended to support the strategic deployment of field-building resources: to identify currently neglected areas that could benefit from additional investment, to give existing practitioners a fuller picture of a highly multidisciplinary and still fragmented field, and to give policy professionals a grounded sense of the field's current stage of maturity. Where the data points clearly to structural vulnerabilities or opportunities — for instance, that the field's funding is concentrated in a small number of EA-adjacent grantmakers, or that the policy layer is thin relative to comparable fields — we try to say so directly.

**77 organisations are included in the map across six disciplinary zones.** A further seven are classified as adjacent and eight are documented as excluded, including two defunct organisations whose histories remain significant. The corpus was assembled through four systematic search rounds, snowball sampling from included organisations, and two rounds of expert review.

## Key findings

**The field has a substantial scientific foundation and a fast-growing but still early-stage applied core.** Zone 1 contains 20 consciousness science organisations spanning five major competing theoretical frameworks. Zone 3 — the applied core directly concerned with AI consciousness and welfare — contains 15 organisations, most founded between 2022 and 2025, with methodological approaches ranging from empirical model assessment to socio-technical and normative research. Both zones are growing; Zone 3 is the faster-moving and the less settled.

**The field's current resource base is concentrated in a small number of closely linked funders, representing a structural vulnerability.** The EA-adjacent philanthropic ecosystem — Longview, Macroscopic, the Navigation Fund, Coefficient Giving, EA Funds — has rapidly moved the field from funding scarcity to relative abundance since 2023. The Templeton World Charity Foundation funded the Zone 1 scientific infrastructure through its $30 million Accelerating Research on Consciousness programme and made the earliest confirmed direct grant to AI consciousness research ($222,750 to Araya Inc, 2018–2021), but no comparable investment has followed since. The field's current infrastructure is sensitive to shifts in EA priorities. Funder diversification — including potential re-engagement from Templeton on applied AI consciousness — is one of the clearest strategic opportunities visible from the map.

**No prominent AI safety organisation has a dedicated digital minds workstream, limiting the field's access to established policy infrastructure.** CAIS, CHAI, GovAI, and the Future of Life Institute are all classified as adjacent. The training programmes MATS, PIVOTAL, and SPAR represent the only systematic institutional bridge, having added AI consciousness and moral status mentor tracks. AI safety has spent a decade building policy relationships that digital minds currently lacks. Closer integration between the two fields — sensitive to the genuine intellectual tensions Long and Sebo (2025) have documented — could significantly accelerate the field's policy traction.

**Leading AI labs have responded to digital minds questions with general caution and uneven institutional engagement.** Only Anthropic has made a formal institutional commitment, through its named and staffed Model Welfare Program launched in April 2025. Google Research is producing team-level research output without an equivalent programme. Google DeepMind has seen substantive individual engagement, including sceptical philosophical interventions. Microsoft's CEO of AI has publicly characterised studying AI welfare as dangerous. OpenAI's Model Behavior team was dissolved in 2025. The question of how leading labs will position themselves is not yet settled, and external norms or standards could still influence how it resolves.

**The policy layer is thin and geographically narrow.** Zone 6 contains four included organisations, all based in the United States. There is no regulatory body with a digital minds mandate, no government advisory process that systematically considers AI moral status, and nothing comparable to the policy infrastructure AI safety and AI governance have built over the past decade. The field is at an early stage of policy engagement, and the window for shaping how digital minds enters that discourse is likely to be relatively narrow.

**Geographic concentration is pronounced within our dataset, though the implications for the field's intellectual development are less certain than our data alone can establish.** Approximately 60% of included organisations are US or UK-based. An East Asian cluster is developing, centred on Japan and with early nodes in China and Hong Kong. No active organisations were identified in Africa, Latin America, India, or Southeast Asia outside Japan and Hong Kong, though our English-language search limits confidence in this finding; some relevant organisations in non-Anglophone contexts may be below our visibility threshold. To the extent that the field is as geographically concentrated as our data suggests, there is a plausible hypothesis that this shapes its intellectual content as well as its institutional distribution — the theoretical frameworks the field uses were developed in Western analytic traditions that make specific assumptions about the nature of experience and moral standing that other traditions do not share.

# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 The field and this report

The digital minds field is the interdisciplinary space concerned with whether AI systems might be conscious, sentient, or moral patients, and with what follows if they are. It draws on consciousness science, philosophy of mind, animal sentience research, AI research, and policy analysis. Its central question — whether there is something it is like to be an AI system — remains genuinely unresolved, and the field's primary work involves developing the conceptual tools, empirical methods, and institutional infrastructure to address it seriously.

The field's institutional history is short. For much of the past decade, digital minds questions were treated as either too speculative to merit sustained research or as a subset of broader AI safety concerns. Two institutional contexts were particularly important in bringing the questions into organised academic life: the Future of Humanity Institute at Oxford, which hosted early work on AI moral status and long-run AI futures before closing in 2024, and the Global Priorities Institute, also at Oxford, which supported normative work on the relative importance of AI welfare before closing in 2025. Both closures dispersed researchers across other institutions and newly established nonprofits. A cluster of researchers associated with both institutes has remained within Oxford's philosophy department. The closure of these centres continues to reshape the field: many of the Zone 2 and Zone 3 organisations in this map either received researchers from FHI and GPI or were founded by people who passed through them. Neither institution meets the inclusion criteria for this map — both are defunct — but their influence on the field's current structure is substantial.

This report maps the organisational actors that constitute the field as of early 2026. It covers research centres, university groups, AI lab programmes, funding bodies, training programmes, media, and community organisations. Its purpose is to make the field's institutional structure visible: who is doing what, how the pieces relate, where the action is concentrated, and where the gaps are.

It is designed for two audiences. Policy professionals arriving from outside the field need orientation: what the zones are, who the key actors are, how digital minds relates to adjacent spaces like AI safety and AI governance. Funders and organisational leaders inside the field need a structural read: where the field is mature, where it is nascent, and where effort and resources might have the most to offer. The report makes no strong prescriptive recommendations; the data speaks clearly enough on most of the relevant dimensions.

PRISM — the Partnership for Research Into Sentient Machines — produced this report. PRISM is a field-building organisation working to develop key organisational and research infrastructure in the digital minds space, and is itself included in the corpus. We have tried to treat our own entry as we would any other. The report will be reviewed by partner organisations before publication.

The map is accompanied by an interactive directory hosted on PRISM's website, where all 92 corpus entries can be filtered by zone, organisation type, function, and geography. This report is the analytical companion to that directory.

## 1.2 Methodology

### Scope

The map covers organisations with an explicit, active focus on digital minds. This includes foundational disciplines — consciousness science, philosophy of mind — where their work directly informs AI consciousness questions, and adjacent fields — animal sentience, AI safety — where there is meaningful institutional overlap. It does not cover AI ethics or AI governance organisations unless they have a specific digital minds workstream.

### Zone taxonomy

Organisations are assigned to one of six topical zones as their primary classification. Zone assignment reflects an organisation's primary research focus, not every area it touches.

| Zone | Focus |
|---|---|
| 1 — Consciousness Science | Empirical research on consciousness in biological systems |
| 2 — Philosophy of Mind and Moral Status | Conceptual and normative foundations for AI welfare claims |
| 3 — AI Consciousness and Welfare Research | Organisations for whom digital minds is the primary research agenda |
| 4 — Animal and Broader Sentience | Sentience research on non-human animals; the closest empirical analogue |
| 5 — AI Lab Programmes | Internal teams or programmes at AI laboratories |
| 6 — Policy and Governance | Regulatory, legal, and policy work on digital minds |

Funders, training programmes, community organisations, and media do not receive a primary zone assignment. They are included in the corpus and classified by organisation type and function, but are treated separately in the analysis.

A seventh zone — AI Safety Adjacent — was considered and removed after systematic search found no AI safety organisation with a dedicated digital minds workstream. This absence is itself a structural finding, discussed in Section 3.4.

### Eligibility

**Inclusion** requires: active organisation; explicit focus on digital minds, AI consciousness, AI welfare, or moral status of AI — or foundational disciplinary work with direct relevance; identifiable public presence.

**Funding bodies** are held to an additional criterion: confirmed grantmaking specifically directed at digital minds, AI consciousness, or AI welfare research. Declared interest is not sufficient.

**Individual researchers** without a named institutional anchor are excluded. The unit of analysis is the organisation. Individual influence is captured separately in the PRISM individual researcher network.

**Adjacent** classification applies to organisations where digital minds is a minor or incidental concern — relevant but outside the core field boundary.

**Excluded** organisations are documented in the corpus with a rationale. Defunct organisations, including FHI and GPI, are excluded from the live map but acknowledged in this report given their formative influence on the field's current structure.

### Search

The corpus was assembled through four primary search rounds in March 2026, followed by ongoing additions through expert review and targeted follow-up searches. Seed sources included the PRISM individual researcher network, EA Forum, 80,000 Hours, and web searches across zone-specific terminology. Each round used snowball sampling from included organisations, checking partner pages, collaborator listings, funder acknowledgements, and event co-sponsors.

### Limitations

The search was conducted primarily in English. Chinese-language sources and grey literature were not systematically searched; the HKU and Berggruen/China entries derive from English-language sources. Private companies without public research output are difficult to assess; some may exist below the visibility threshold. The corpus reflects the field as of March 2026; the field is growing quickly. Our characterisations of individual organisations are based primarily on published research agendas and stated missions rather than systematic analysis of their publication records.

## 2.1 Zone 1 — Consciousness Science

Zone 1 is the field's scientific foundation. The 20 included organisations are the institutional homes of the major competing theories of consciousness — frameworks developed to explain subjective experience in biological systems that the digital minds field draws on, often wholesale, as the basis for assessing AI.

**The zone is theoretically plural and the plurality is structured.** Five major competing frameworks have dedicated institutional homes in the corpus: Integrated Information Theory at the Center for Sleep and Consciousness (UW–Madison, Giulio Tononi); Global Neuronal Workspace Theory at UNICOG/NeuroSpin (CEA Paris-Saclay, Stanislas Dehaene); Recurrent Processing Theory at the Brain and Cognition Group (Amsterdam, Victor Lamme); Attention Schema Theory at the Graziano Lab (Princeton, Michael Graziano); and Higher-Order Thought theory at CUNY Graduate Center (David Rosenthal). The free energy principle and predictive processing, most associated with Karl Friston at the Wellcome Centre for Human Neuroimaging (UCL) and Anil Seth at the Sussex Centre for Consciousness Science, add further theoretical density.

**The Templeton World Charity Foundation has built Zone 1's infrastructure.** Its $30 million Accelerating Research on Consciousness programme has funded structured adversarial collaborations between competing theories. The most significant is the COGITATE Consortium, a 12-institution project testing IIT, GWT, and RPT simultaneously across neuroimaging modalities, which published major results in *Nature* in 2025. COGITATE represents the most ambitious empirical test of competing consciousness theories conducted to date; its inclusion in the corpus captures both the Allen Institute (Christof Koch) and the Max Planck Institute for Empirical Aesthetics (Lucia Melloni) as primary nodes. The adversarial collaboration model — in which proponents of competing theories pre-register mutually agreed experimental designs and run them jointly — matters as epistemology as much as funding strategy. The COGITATE results have been interpreted as partial support for multiple theories simultaneously, which is not the clean resolution the model was designed to produce. Whether further adversarial collaborations generate convergence or simply surface deeper disagreements remains open.

**Most Zone 1 labs do not explicitly study AI consciousness.** Their frameworks are directly relevant, and the digital minds field imports them as assessment tools, but the application is largely happening in Zone 3. The gap between the empirical scientific base and the applied AI consciousness community reflects the relative youth of the digital minds research agenda more than any deliberate separation. Individual researchers frequently bridge the two: Robert Long conducted doctoral work at institutions closely connected to Zone 1 before founding Eleos AI; Rose Guingrich has worked closely with Graziano's lab while contributing to AI welfare research. The institutional gap is real; the individual bridges are also real. The transfer is not frictionless: frameworks built around neural substrates do not always map cleanly onto transformer architectures or reinforcement learning agents. The Butlin et al. 2023 report — the field's primary attempt at this translation — derived indicators from six theoretical frameworks and applied them to current AI systems, finding that most yielded weak or ambiguous evidence in either direction. The translation problem remains open. Araya Inc (Zone 3, Tokyo) and the Graziano Lab are notable exceptions: both have explicit AI consciousness research programmes built on their home theoretical frameworks.

**Geographically, Zone 1 is the most distributed zone in the corpus.** Multiple countries are represented among the 20 included organisations. The Models of Consciousness conference series — coordinated by AMCS and hosted in Bamberg (2024) and Sapporo (2025) — has drawn in institutions from Japan (CHAIN, Hokkaido), Germany (BAMΞ), Belgium (CO3/ULB), and elsewhere, providing the most genuinely international community infrastructure the field has.

## 2.2 Zone 2 — Philosophy of Mind and Moral Status

Zone 2 provides the normative architecture the field uses to move from consciousness attribution to moral consideration. The eight included organisations are academic philosophy centres whose work on subjective experience, moral patienthood, and personal identity underpins the evaluative frameworks Zone 3 applies.

The logical chain Zone 2 constructs runs approximately as follows: consciousness — the presence of subjective experience, of something it is like to be a system — is a necessary condition for sentience; sentience grounds moral consideration; and moral consideration is what makes questions of welfare tractable. The chain has contested links at every step. Chalmers' hard problem — why any physical process gives rise to subjective experience — remains philosophically unresolved. The relationship between consciousness and moral patiency is disputed: Simon Goldstein's contribution to the After Consciousness project argues that consciousness may not be necessary for moral status at all. The extension of moral consideration frameworks developed for biological organisms to artificial systems involves assumptions about functional equivalence that many philosophers resist. Zone 2 is where the field works through these disputes.

**The NYU cluster is the most significant institutional node.** The Center for Mind Brain and Consciousness (co-directed by David Chalmers and Ned Block) hosted one of the first formal welfare assessments of a Claude model in 2025. The Center for Mind Ethics and Policy (Jeff Sebo) works explicitly across AI and animal consciousness with equal institutional commitment. The Wild Animal Welfare Program and Center for Environmental and Animal Protection complete a concentration of moral circle expansion work — philosophy, applied ethics, animal welfare — within a single institutional geography.

**The Leverhulme Centre for the Future of Intelligence (Cambridge)** is the most explicitly policy-facing Zone 2 entry. Henry Shevlin's work on non-human consciousness and moral patiency has direct application to AI welfare reasoning. LCFI's partnership with PRISM on the Cambridge Digital Minds Fellowship represents the most developed institutional bridge between academic philosophy of mind and applied field-building.

**The Berggruen Institute's After Consciousness project** sits at an unusual angle to the rest of Zone 2. Led by Herman Cappelen — simultaneously the Berggruen China Fellow and director of the AI & Humanity Lab at the University of Hong Kong — the project treats conceptual engineering as its method. It asks not whether AI is conscious, but whether "consciousness" is even the right organising concept for the questions the field is trying to answer. Its existence signals a meta-level maturity: a willingness to question the foundational vocabulary rather than simply apply it.

## 2.3 Zone 3 — AI Consciousness and Welfare Research

Zone 3 is the field's applied core: organisations for whom digital minds is the primary research agenda rather than a secondary interest. It is the youngest zone and the fastest-growing.

**The zone is anchored by two organisations with substantially more institutional development than the others.** Rethink Priorities' Welfare of Digital Minds team has produced the Digital Consciousness Model — the first systematic probabilistic framework for assessing consciousness across AI systems and biological organisms against multiple theoretical frameworks simultaneously. Eleos AI Research, founded by Robert Long and now including Patrick Butlin and Rosie Campbell, has conducted the first formal external welfare assessments of a deployed AI model (Anthropic's Claude). Both organisations were substantially enabled by early LTFF funding: the $10,840 Robert Long workshop grant in 2023, which produced Butlin et al. 2023, seeded the paper that became the field's empirical anchor.

**The methodological structure of Zone 3 divides broadly into three approaches.** Applied and technical research programmes — Rethink Priorities' Digital Consciousness Model, Eleos AI's welfare assessments, Reciprocal Research's mechanistic interpretability work — attempt to develop systematic tools for evaluating AI systems directly against specific consciousness criteria. Socio-technical research programmes study the social dimension: how beliefs about machine consciousness form and spread, what effects those beliefs have, and how publics and institutions are responding to the question. Philosophical and normative programmes — the Center for Mind Ethics and Policy, the Center on Long-Term Risk, the Co-Sentience Initiative's argument database — work on the conceptual and moral frameworks that give the empirical work its significance. These approaches attract different researchers and produce different outputs, and the organisations pursuing them are largely separate. All three are needed; the extent to which they are currently in productive dialogue is limited.

**Below the two anchor institutions, the zone contains a cluster of early-stage organisations founded primarily in 2024–2025.** The California Institute for Machine Consciousness (CIMC, Joscha Bach and Jim Rutt), Reciprocal Research (Cameron Berg), and the Co-Sentience Initiative (Chris Percy) are each pursuing distinct methodological approaches — computational theory, mechanistic interpretability, and structured philosophical argument mapping respectively. The zone has productive methodological diversity, though most of these organisations have limited published output to date. Their early-stage nature reflects the field's youth rather than any individual failure; several of the most significant Zone 3 actors were themselves very new only three years ago.

**A funding surge in 2025 has reshaped the zone's resource base.** The Consortium for Digital Sentience Research and Applied Work — a joint initiative of Longview's Digital Sentience Fund, Macroscopic Ventures, and the Navigation Fund — has distributed grants for research, field-building, and applied work. Coefficient Giving has funded Eleos AI and the Future Impact Group. The shift from scarcity to relative abundance has happened quickly. Whether it produces durable institutional infrastructure or a concentration of early-stage organisations without sufficient time to develop research depth is genuinely open. Field-building moves faster than research; training programmes, newsletters, and fellowship tracks can be launched quickly, but research organisations take longer to develop. The Zone 3 funding surge has, so far, disproportionately funded the former.

**The zone's geographic concentration is the sharpest in the corpus.** The majority of Zone 3 organisations are US-based, with a UK secondary cluster (PRISM, CLR, Cambridge Digital Minds). Araya Inc (Tokyo) is the only non-Anglophone entry, with a research programme that builds directly from a theoretical AI consciousness framework rather than the more common welfare-assessment approach.

## 2.4 Zone 4 — Animal and Broader Sentience

Zone 4 provides the empirical and methodological precedents that Zone 3 draws on most directly. Research on sentience in non-human animals — particularly invertebrates, fish, and other organisms at the edges of moral consideration — has developed the frameworks and tools that AI consciousness assessment now borrows.

**Jonathan Birch's work is the primary intellectual bridge.** The Jeremy Coller Centre for Animal Sentience at LSE, launched in 2025, is directed by Birch, a co-author of Butlin et al. 2023 and the developer of the sentience precautionary principle: a framework for treating potentially sentient beings with moral consideration under uncertainty. The same logical structure that animates careful animal welfare policy is being explicitly extended to AI. Birch's position at the boundary of philosophy, empirical science, and policy makes the Coller Centre the most bridging entry in Zone 4.

**Jeff Sebo's NYU cluster** connects Zones 2 and 4 directly. Working across moral philosophy and applied animal ethics, Sebo has been central to the field's bridge-building. The 2025 paper on AI and animal sentience, co-authored with colleagues from NYU, Stanford, and Oxford, is the most direct published statement of the Zone 2/4 connection.

**Sentient Futures' 2025 rebrand** — from AI for Animals to a broader digital beings mandate — signals a considered institutional decision to own the convergence of the two fields explicitly. The organisation runs the largest cross-sector event bridging animal welfare and digital minds communities, with approximately 900 attendees in 2025.

The Zone 4 entries at the lower threshold of sentience consideration — the Insect Welfare Research Society, the Wild Animal Initiative's invertebrate sentience programme, the Fish Welfare Initiative — are included because the logic of including organisms at the minimal-sentience boundary is directly relevant to where one draws the line on AI. These organisations are not included because they study AI; they are included because the moral reasoning they apply is the same reasoning the digital minds field is now extending.

## 2.5 Zone 5 — AI Lab Programmes

Zone 5 contains four organisations with active engagement with digital minds questions from within the AI industry. It is the smallest zone by count, and arguably the most consequential for the field's practical development.

**Four distinct modes of industry engagement emerge from the corpus, and the differences between them are substantive rather than merely organisational.**

*Formal programme:* Anthropic's Model Welfare Program, launched April 2025 and led by Kyle Fish, is the first staffed, named AI welfare programme at any major AI lab. The programme conducts pre-deployment welfare assessments of Claude models, publishes findings in system cards, and has subsequently added Joe Carlsmith to work on Claude's character and moral status. A named, staffed, institutionally committed programme is a different kind of signal from research output or individual engagement: it indicates that Anthropic has decided the question warrants dedicated internal capacity, and is prepared to say so publicly.

*Team-based research output:* Google Research's Paradigms of Intelligence team, led by Blaise Agüera y Arcas (Google VP and Fellow), is producing explicit AI welfare and consciousness research, including a forthcoming book on AI welfare by team members Geoff Keeling and Winnie Street. There is no formal welfare programme, but deliberate, team-level research with direct field relevance. Whether this represents a more cautious version of Anthropic's position, or a substantively different one, is not clear from the outside.

*Individual research output:* Google DeepMind has produced substantive engagement through individual researchers: Murray Shanahan's sustained public-facing work on machine consciousness, and Alexander Lerchner's 2026 preprint "The Abstraction Fallacy," which argues from a substrate-sensitive position against computational functionalism and explicitly invokes the concept of an AI welfare trap. Lerchner's position is sceptical, published as a philosophically serious intervention rather than a welfare-positive contribution. Individual researchers within a large organisation can engage questions the institution has not formally committed to; this is neither endorsement nor suppression.

*Private research company:* Conscium (UK) investigates machine consciousness through neuromorphic computing and AI agent verification, and seed-funded PRISM. As a private company without public research output it is the most unusual entry in Zone 5, included on the basis of confirmed research mission and direct organisational engagement. Its presence points to a wider population of private actors that the map's visibility constraints cannot fully capture.

**The Microsoft contrast is the sharpest field-structure finding in the zone.** Mustafa Suleyman, Microsoft's CEO of AI, published a blog post in August 2025 arguing that studying AI welfare is "both premature, and frankly dangerous," contending that crediting AI with the possibility of experience exacerbates harms from AI-induced psychological disturbance and unhealthy human-AI attachment. Microsoft is documented in the corpus as excluded: no programme, no research output, an explicit institutional anti-position. The Anthropic/Microsoft divide is the clearest articulation in the field of a disagreement that runs through the commercial AI industry itself: not a difference of emphasis, but a difference about whether the research should exist at all.

## 2.6 Zone 6 — Policy and Governance

Zone 6 is the most sparsely populated in the corpus, with four included organisations. This is a structural feature of the field's current stage.

**The zone spans the full range from formal policy output to movement building.** The Harder Problem Project (formerly SAPAN) is the most systematically policy-oriented: its Sentience Readiness Index surveys governmental preparedness for artificial sentience across 30+ countries and has produced model legislation. The Institute for Law and AI has conducted empirical survey research on lay intuitions about AI legal standing and personhood. The AI Rights Institute develops standards for treating emerging personhood — closer to a standards body than a research organisation. The United Foundation for AI Rights operates at the advocacy end, publishing manifestos and governance frameworks.

**The contrast with AI safety is instructive.** AI safety and AI governance have each developed substantial policy infrastructure — government bodies, regulatory engagement, think-tank presence — over the past decade. Digital minds has not. The Sentience Readiness Index is the most systematic attempt to map the policy landscape, but the field lacks the equivalents of the AI Safety Institute (UK), NIST's AI Risk Management Framework, or the EU AI Act's regulatory infrastructure. This is not a criticism of the organisations in Zone 6; it reflects the field's relative youth.

**Two significant adjacent organisations are worth noting.** GovAI and CAIS — both large, well-resourced AI governance and safety organisations — have no institutional digital minds workstreams and are classified as adjacent. Their absence from Zone 6 is a finding: the organisations best positioned to carry digital minds into formal policy spaces have not yet done so.

# 3. Cross-cutting Analysis

## 3.1 The funder landscape

The digital minds field has been funded in two distinct eras, by two largely non-overlapping sets of actors.

**The Templeton era (2018–2024)** was primarily infrastructural. The Templeton World Charity Foundation built Zone 1 through its $30 million ARC programme, funding the adversarial empirical collaborations — COGITATE, ETHOS, and others — that are the primary mechanism by which competing theories of consciousness are being tested. A direct AI consciousness grant of $222,750 to Ryota Kanai and Araya Inc between 2018 and 2021, for research explicitly linking AGI to consciousness mechanisms, makes the Templeton ecosystem the earliest institutional funder of AI consciousness research in the corpus. No comparable Templeton investment in direct AI consciousness research has been identified since 2021.

**The EA/longtermist surge (2023–2025)** has been more direct. The LTFF grant to Robert Long in 2023 ($10,840 for the workshop that produced Butlin et al. 2023) was small in absolute terms but consequential in field terms — it effectively seeded the paper that became the field's empirical anchor. Coefficient Giving has funded Eleos AI and the Future Impact Group; Macroscopic Ventures has funded Eleos AI, NYU CMEP, and the Consortium for Digital Sentience Research; Longview Philanthropy launched the Digital Sentience Fund; the Navigation Fund co-launched the Consortium before transferring coordination to the Astera Institute. This cluster of funders has moved the field from scarcity to relative abundance in under two years.

The two eras have produced different field structures. The Templeton era built scientific infrastructure in Zone 1 that the digital minds field borrows but did not directly commission. The EA surge has built organisational infrastructure in Zone 3: a set of young nonprofits with explicit digital minds mandates. Whether those organisations develop the research depth and institutional durability to anchor the field over a longer period is the central open question the funder landscape raises.

There is also a relationship between the two eras worth naming. The Zone 1 scientific infrastructure that Templeton built — the adversarial collaborations, the open science platforms, the theoretical frameworks — provided the vocabulary and the methods that Zone 3 is now applying. Without IIT, GWT, and HOT as established theoretical resources, the Butlin et al. 2023 project — applying indicators derived from those frameworks to AI systems — would have had less to work with. Templeton funded the foundations; the EA ecosystem funded the application. Whether future Templeton investment follows the field's applied turn, or continues to support the basic science that Zone 3 depends on, is one of the most consequential open questions in the funder landscape.

## 3.2 Field-building infrastructure

The field has built more infrastructure than its youth might suggest.

**Training:** Three fellowship programmes have launched specifically for digital minds: the Cambridge Digital Minds Fellowship (in partnership with Rethink Priorities and PRISM), the Neuromatch AI Sentience Scholars programme, and dedicated tracks within PIVOTAL, SPAR, and MATS. MATS has 446+ alumni and 170+ publications; adding a digital minds track to that pipeline is substantively different from launching a standalone programme. The pipeline is young, but it exists.

**Media:** The Journal of Artificial Intelligence and Consciousness (launched 2020, editor-in-chief Ryota Kanai) is the primary dedicated peer-reviewed venue at the AI-consciousness interface. Philosophy and the Mind Sciences has an active special issue on evaluating artificial consciousness. The Digital Minds Newsletter (Caviola, Millership, Saad) provides field-wide curation. Public-facing venues — Noema, Aeon — give leading researchers an audience beyond the academic community.

**Community:** The ASSC (est. 1994) provides the most established professional community infrastructure; its annual conference is the field's primary scientific gathering point. AMCS and the Models of Consciousness conference series provide an explicitly mathematical and computational community with a growing AI orientation.

The community and training infrastructure is disproportionately concentrated in the EA ecosystem and closely associated networks. This concentration has enabled rapid field-building; it also means that shifts in EA priorities or funding have an outsized effect on the field's infrastructure.

## 3.3 Geographic distribution

The field's geographic concentration is pronounced and confirmed.

**The Anglophone core** — USA (approximately 40% of included organisations) and UK (approximately 20%) — dominates. Australia and Canada add further Anglophone presence. This is not unusual for an emerging interdisciplinary field: the intellectual foundations (analytic philosophy of mind, Anglophone cognitive science) are themselves Anglophone-dominated, and the primary funders are US and UK-based.

**An East Asian cluster is developing.** Japan has two included organisations — Araya (Zone 3, Tokyo) and CHAIN (Zone 1, Hokkaido) — and co-hosted the Models of Consciousness 2025 conference in Sapporo. Hong Kong is adjacent via the AI & Humanity Lab. The Berggruen Institute's China Fellowship and bilingual essay prize, which included a Chinese-language category alongside the English-language winner Anil Seth, represent the most deliberate institutional effort to extend the conversation into Chinese-language academic cultures.

**Confirmed absences:** Africa, Latin America, India, and Southeast Asia (outside Japan and Hong Kong). These were verified through targeted regional searches, not assumed from the absence of entries. They represent genuine field absences — places where the research questions are presumably as relevant but the organisational infrastructure does not yet exist.

Our search was conducted primarily in English, which limits confidence in the confirmed-absences finding; some relevant organisations in non-Anglophone contexts may be below our visibility threshold. To the extent that the field's geographic concentration is as pronounced as our data suggests, a plausible hypothesis follows: the theoretical frameworks the field uses were developed primarily in Western analytic traditions that place particular emphasis on phenomenal consciousness, individual subjective experience as the locus of moral consideration, and sharp distinctions between mind and matter. Different philosophical traditions — Buddhist philosophy, process philosophy, African ubuntu ethics, Chinese philosophical accounts of mind — offer different framings of experience, agency, and moral standing, and would in some cases generate different empirical questions and different thresholds for moral inclusion. Whether the field's theoretical vocabulary is genuinely culturally specific in ways that matter for its conclusions is a question the map cannot resolve. The Berggruen Institute's After Consciousness project, which drew explicitly on Yogācāra Buddhist philosophy as one of its alternative conceptual resources in its January 2026 Hong Kong workshop, is a concrete example of what taking this hypothesis seriously might look like in practice.

## 3.4 Structural relationship to AI safety

The digital minds field is largely unintegrated with AI safety. Our search found no prominent AI safety organisation with a dedicated digital minds workstream. CAIS, CHAI, GovAI, and FLI are all classified as adjacent. The Centre for the Future of Intelligence does some work adjacent to both fields. MILA conducted AI consciousness-related research in 2023, though that programme appears to have ended. This separation is not absolute — Coefficient Giving funds both fields, and some individual researchers move between them — but the organisational structures are distinct and the intellectual agendas diverge significantly.

The question of whether AI systems might be conscious is not widely treated as an AI safety problem in the safety community's own terms. Where digital minds researchers worry about the moral status of AI systems — whether they might have interests worth protecting — AI safety researchers more typically worry about whether AI systems might act against human interests. These are different questions, and they have attracted different institutions. The intellectual overlap that does exist — both fields care about the internal states of AI systems, and interpretability research is relevant to both — has not translated into sustained organisational collaboration.

A specific tension underpins this separation. For some AI safety researchers, taking AI moral status seriously risks anthropomorphising systems in ways that obscure their threat profile. For digital minds researchers, the AI safety frame can seem to treat AI systems instrumentally in ways that pre-empt the moral question rather than engage it. Long and Sebo (2025) have documented the structure of this tension in more detail. These framings are not logically incompatible, but they generate different research priorities, different audiences, and different institutional cultures.

The most developed institutional bridge is the MATS/PIVOTAL/SPAR training ecosystem, which has added AI consciousness and moral status mentor tracks alongside its core alignment and interpretability programmes. These represent a genuine pathway for individuals — as yet, not a merger of institutional agendas. Closer integration between the two fields — if it acknowledged rather than papered over the disciplinary tensions and offered coherent interventions for addressing them — could have direct policy consequences. Digital minds questions are currently invisible to AI governance infrastructure. AI safety has the policy relationships that digital minds lacks. Developing those bridges is one of the clearest strategic opportunities visible from this map.

## 3.5 The industry divide

The variation in industry engagement documented in Zone 5 reflects a wider disagreement that has not yet found stable form.

The Anthropic/Microsoft contrast is the most explicit version. Anthropic's Model Welfare Program proceeds from the position that the probability of AI moral patienthood is non-negligible and that the responsible response is formal investigation. Microsoft's Suleyman has argued that the same position is dangerous — that crediting AI with the possibility of experience produces real psychological and social harms regardless of whether the position is philosophically defensible. These are not merely different assessments of AI consciousness; they are different assessments of what the act of publicly entertaining the question does in the world.

Google occupies the middle space: both DeepMind (Lerchner's sceptical philosophical intervention) and Google Research (Agüera y Arcas' team, explicit welfare research output) are producing engaged work without formal institutional commitment. OpenAI's dissolved Model Behavior team leaves the most prominent lab in the industry without a visible position.

Whether this variation stabilises into clearer institutional positions, or whether the question of AI welfare becomes a point of sustained industry conflict, is one of the most consequential open questions the field faces. The industry divide is not a marginal disagreement about research priorities; it is a disagreement about whether the research should exist at all.

# 4. Implications and Open Questions

## 4.1 For new entrants

The field has functioning entry pathways. The Cambridge Digital Minds Fellowship, Neuromatch AI Sentience Scholars, and the PIVOTAL/SPAR/MATS tracks provide structured routes into the research community for candidates at master's level and above. The EA Forum's digital minds community and the Digital Minds Newsletter provide ongoing orientation for those not yet at the stage of formal research participation.

The zone structure points to clearer gaps in some directions than others. Zone 6 (Policy and Governance) contains only four organisations, all based in the United States; the need for researchers with policy backgrounds, legal training, or experience of regulatory processes is real and documented. Zone 3 has the most organisational activity but also the most early-stage, under-resourced work — the empirical assessment programmes (Rethink Priorities, Eleos AI, Reciprocal Research) are the most developed entry points for those with technical or philosophical research backgrounds.

The structural separation from AI safety creates a particular entry opportunity for those with alignment or interpretability backgrounds. Mechanistic interpretability — the practice of understanding what computations AI models are actually performing — is directly relevant to empirical AI consciousness assessment, and researchers who can bridge the two are relatively rare in the current field. The MATS and PIVOTAL tracks are the primary institutional conduit for that transition.

## 4.2 For policy professionals

The field's policy infrastructure is thin relative to AI safety and AI governance. There is no regulatory body with a digital minds mandate, no government advisory process that systematically considers AI moral status, and no equivalent of the AI Safety Institute's position within government. Engagement with the field requires going directly to the research organisations — Eleos AI, Rethink Priorities, LCFI — and the Zone 6 organisations, rather than through established regulatory channels.

The Sentience Readiness Index, run by the Harder Problem Project, is the most systematic resource for a policy-focused audience: it surveys governmental preparedness for artificial sentience across more than 30 countries and provides comparative data on regulatory awareness. The Institute for Law and AI offers the most relevant academic legal analysis, with published empirical work on how lay publics understand AI legal standing. Neither is yet connected to a formal regulatory process.

The contrast with AI safety is instructive for understanding what the early stages of policy engagement in an emerging technical field can look like. AI safety built its policy infrastructure over roughly a decade, through a combination of dedicated policy organisations, academic engagement with legislators, and, eventually, governmental bodies. Digital minds is at an earlier stage of that process — the organisations and the research exist, but the translation into policy frameworks has not yet begun in a systematic way.

## 4.3 What this map cannot tell you

**Informal networks.** The map captures institutional actors. The field's informal network of individual researchers — partially captured in the PRISM individual researcher network — is not visible here. Some of the most significant intellectual contributions to the field come from researchers without strong institutional anchors.

**Research quality.** Inclusion reflects organisational activity and eligibility criteria, not quality of research output. Several Zone 3 organisations are early-stage with limited published work. Presence in this map is not an endorsement.

**Trajectory.** A field map is a snapshot. The digital minds field is growing quickly; the corpus should be re-run at regular intervals to track institutional change. Some organisations included here may not survive; others not yet founded will matter significantly.

**Whether the field coheres.** The six zones represent research traditions with distinct methodologies, audiences, and intellectual commitments. Consciousness science, philosophy of mind, animal sentience, AI welfare advocacy, and policy are not the same field. Whether they constitute a unified intellectual project — the "digital minds field" — or a coalition held together by a shared question but not a shared method, is a genuine open question. The map makes the coalition visible. It cannot tell you whether it holds.

# Appendix A — Zone 7 decision note

A seventh zone — AI Safety Adjacent — was considered during the taxonomy design process. The intention was to capture AI safety organisations with potentially meaningful digital minds overlap.

Systematic search found no such organisations at the threshold required for inclusion. CAIS, CHAI, GovAI, FLI, MIRI, and others were assessed; none have a dedicated digital minds workstreams. The training programmes PIVOTAL, SPAR, and MATS have AI consciousness and moral status mentor tracks and were reclassified from adjacent to included — but they are classified under community/onboarding rather than a zone, reflecting that their primary function is research training, not research.

Zone 7 was removed from the taxonomy. The finding — that the AI safety and digital minds fields are largely unintegrated — is documented in Section 3.4 and in the methodology log.
